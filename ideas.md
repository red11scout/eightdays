## Idea 1: "Illuminated Manuscript" — Medieval Codex Reimagined

**Design Movement**: Neo-Medieval / Illuminated Manuscript meets modern digital typography

**Core Principles**:
1. Sacred geometry and structured hierarchy — content flows like a codex page with ornamental borders and decorated initials
2. Warm parchment textures with deep jewel-tone accents — evoking candlelit scriptoriums
3. Vertical narrative scrolling — the user "unrolls" the week like a scroll
4. Reverence through restraint — ornament serves meaning, never decoration alone

**Color Philosophy**: A warm, aged parchment base (#F5ECD7) grounded by deep burgundy (#6B1D2A), antique gold (#B8860B), and monastery ink (#2C1810). The palette evokes sacred texts, stained glass, and candlelight. Gold is used sparingly as an accent for divine moments (prophecy fulfillments, Jesus' words).

**Layout Paradigm**: A single-column "codex" layout with generous margins. Each day is a "chapter" with a decorated initial capital. Sidebar marginalia appears on wider screens showing cross-references and prophecy links. The timeline runs vertically like a vine or tree of Jesse along the left edge.

**Signature Elements**:
- Ornamental dividers between sections using SVG patterns inspired by Celtic/medieval knotwork
- "Illuminated" drop caps at the start of each day's narrative
- Gold-leaf accent lines that trace prophecy connections

**Interaction Philosophy**: Slow, deliberate scrolling. Sections reveal with gentle fade-ins. Clicking a prophecy reference highlights both the OT source and NT fulfillment simultaneously. The experience should feel contemplative, not hurried.

**Animation**: Subtle parallax on parchment textures. Drop caps scale in gently. Tables materialize row by row. No flashy transitions — everything breathes.

**Typography System**: Cinzel (display/headings) paired with Crimson Pro (body text). Cinzel evokes Roman inscriptions and sacred authority. Crimson Pro provides excellent readability with a classical serif character.

## Idea 2: "Sacred Cartography" — The Journey Mapped

**Design Movement**: Editorial Cartographic / National Geographic meets devotional guide

**Core Principles**:
1. The week as a journey — navigation is spatial and temporal, like tracing a path on a map
2. Layered information density — overview first, then drill into each day
3. Earth-toned warmth with strategic crimson — the land of Israel meets the blood of the cross
4. Documentary authority — the tone of a definitive reference work

**Color Philosophy**: Sandy stone (#D4C5A9) and warm white (#FAF8F3) as base. Deep olive (#3D4F2F) for primary text and navigation. Crimson (#9B2335) for emphasis and the Friday section. Dusty blue (#6B8BA4) for water/sky references and Thursday's upper room. The palette is drawn from the Judean landscape.

**Layout Paradigm**: A horizontal timeline "map" at the top that serves as primary navigation — each day is a waypoint. Below, content unfolds in an editorial magazine layout with asymmetric two-column grids, pull quotes in the margins, and full-bleed section dividers. Tables are styled as field-guide reference cards.

**Signature Elements**:
- A persistent horizontal journey bar showing progress through the week with waypoint markers
- "Field guide" reference cards for prophecy tables with torn-edge paper effects
- Topographic contour line patterns as section backgrounds

**Interaction Philosophy**: The user is a pilgrim walking through the week. The journey bar tracks position. Clicking a day jumps to that section with a smooth scroll. Prophecy cards can be expanded/collapsed. The experience rewards exploration.

**Animation**: The journey bar fills as you scroll. Section headers slide in from the direction of travel. Cards lift on hover with subtle shadow depth. Contour lines drift slowly in backgrounds.

**Typography System**: Playfair Display (headings) paired with Source Serif 4 (body). Playfair provides editorial gravitas. Source Serif 4 offers superb readability at all sizes with a warm, humanist character.

## Idea 3: "Stained Glass" — Light Through Darkness

**Design Movement**: Art Nouveau Sacred / Stained Glass Modernism

**Core Principles**:
1. Light as narrative — the week moves from bright (Sunday) through darkness (Friday) to radiant glory (Easter)
2. Bold color fields separated by dark leading lines — like stained glass panels
3. Each day has its own color identity within a unified chromatic arc
4. The cross as structural element — vertical and horizontal lines organize the layout

**Color Philosophy**: Each day gets a dominant hue: Sunday (emerald green #2D6A4F), Monday (amber #D4A017), Tuesday (burnt orange #CC5500), Wednesday (slate gray #5A6672), Thursday (deep purple #4A1A6B), Friday (crimson-to-black gradient #8B0000→#1A0000), Saturday (charcoal #333333), Easter Sunday (brilliant gold-to-white #FFD700→#FFFFFF). Dark "leading" borders (#1A1A1A) separate sections.

**Layout Paradigm**: Full-viewport sections for each day, stacked vertically. Each section is a "panel" with its own color world. Within panels, content uses a cruciform grid — a vertical spine with horizontal cross-beams for tables and sidebars. Navigation is a vertical strip of colored dots on the side.

**Signature Elements**:
- Color-coded day panels that shift the entire viewport palette
- Dark "leading line" borders between all content blocks
- A vertical dot navigation strip showing the chromatic arc of the week

**Interaction Philosophy**: Immersive and dramatic. Each day is a world. Scrolling between days is a transition between color environments. The Friday section darkens progressively. Easter Sunday bursts with light. The user feels the emotional weight of the narrative.

**Animation**: Color transitions between sections are smooth and cinematic. Text appears with a gentle glow effect. The Friday section has a slow vignette darkening. Easter Sunday has a radial light burst on entry.

**Typography System**: Cormorant Garamond (display) paired with Lora (body). Cormorant Garamond has an elegant, slightly condensed quality perfect for sacred headings. Lora provides warm, balanced readability with subtle calligraphic roots.

## Selected Approach: Idea 2 — "Sacred Cartography"

I'm selecting the **Sacred Cartography** approach. It best serves the study's purpose as a shareable community guide — the editorial magazine layout handles dense tabular content elegantly, the journey metaphor makes navigation intuitive, and the earth-toned palette feels warm and inviting without being heavy. The horizontal timeline provides an immediate overview of the entire week, making it easy for readers to orient themselves and jump to specific days.
